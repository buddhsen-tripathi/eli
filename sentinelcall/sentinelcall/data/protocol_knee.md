# Post-Op Recovery Reference Protocol — Total Knee Replacement

> **LAYER 2 — REFERENCE (general).** This is clinician-authored *general*
> guidance about what is normal and what is a red flag during knee-replacement
> recovery. It is the ONLY layer that is RAG'd. It informs the agent's
> questions and escalation logic. It is **never** read to the patient as a
> recommendation, and it never overrides the patient's own prescribed record
> (Layer 1). Prescribed doses, dates, and thresholds are quoted verbatim from
> the patient record — never from this document.

## What is normal in the first two weeks

- **Pain** that gradually improves day over day, well-controlled by prescribed
  medication. Some pain with physical-therapy exercises is expected.
- **Swelling and bruising** around the knee and down the leg. Mild swelling can
  persist for weeks. Elevation and ice help.
- **Warmth** of the skin over the incision that is mild and not spreading.
- **Mild itching** as the incision heals, especially around day 7 and later.
- A **small amount of clear or lightly blood-tinged fluid** from the incision in
  the first 24–72 hours.
- **Trouble sleeping** and reduced appetite in the first several days.
- **Numbness** in a patch of skin to the outside of the incision — common and
  usually improves over months.

## Red flags — route to a nurse (never diagnosed by the agent)

### Infection signals
- **Fever of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher.**
- **Spreading redness** that extends outward from the incision (e.g. beyond
  ~2 cm), especially if it is growing day to day.
- **Pus, cloudy, or foul-smelling drainage** from the wound.
- **Increasing warmth** combined with increasing pain and redness.
- **Wound edges opening** or separating.
- Chills or feeling generally unwell alongside any of the above.

### Blood clot signals (DVT / PE)
- **New or increasing calf pain, tenderness, or swelling**, especially in one
  leg — possible deep vein thrombosis. Route to clinical review.
- **Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain** — this is an EMERGENCY, not a
  routine red flag. Instruct the patient to call 911.

### Pain / medication signals
- **Pain rated 8 or higher** that is not controlled by the prescribed regimen,
  or pain that is suddenly much worse than the day before.
- **Confusion about medication** — taking two pain medications together,
  double-dosing, or missing blood-thinner doses. Route to nurse/pharmacy.

### Falls and mobility
- **Any fall, or a near-fall** where the patient had to catch themselves.
- Sudden inability to bear weight that was previously tolerated.

### Cognitive / delirium signals (passive listen)
- New **confusion, disorientation, difficulty finding words**, or a caregiver
  reporting the patient "seems off today." In older post-op patients this can
  be an early sign of delirium or infection and warrants a nurse assessment.

## Emergency — instruct 911 immediately (pre-LLM screen handles these)

- Chest pain or pressure.
- Trouble breathing / shortness of breath.
- Uncontrolled bleeding that will not stop with pressure.
- A fall in which the patient cannot get up or may have hit their head.
- Sudden weakness, face drooping, or slurred speech (possible stroke).

## Day-by-day orientation (general)

- **Day 1–2:** Focus on pain control, ankle pumps, and getting up with help.
  Some drainage and a lot of swelling are expected.
- **Day 3–5:** Walking short distances with a walker. Redness should not be
  spreading; drainage should be decreasing.
- **Day 6–10:** Increasing mobility, possibly advancing to a cane. Itching as
  the incision heals is common. Staples/strips often removed around this window
  at the follow-up visit.
- **Week 2+:** Continued strengthening. Persistent swelling is still normal;
  new spreading redness, fever, or calf pain never is.
